The press-check problem

Reviewers don't read drafts. They mark them. The interface should reflect that — and most don't.

A galley is a particular kind of physical artifact. It is a full-resolution proof, identical in width and column-rule to the eventual broadsheet, presented on paper one weight heavier than the final stock. The reviewer's job is not to read it; their job is to find what is wrong before the run. The medium tells them so. The wide right margin is empty by design — it is where the red pencil goes.

What we call a "review surface" is rarely a galley. It is usually a reading view with affordances bolted on: a sidebar of comments two viewports away from the prose they annotate, a button labeled Reviewer Mode that pops a chrome bar at the top, an emoji-laden thread that reads more like Slack than like ink. None of that is wrong, exactly. None of it is right.

What a galley actually is

The press-check tradition has very old conventions. The galley sits on a desk under a pool of light. The reviewer holds a red pencil — actual red, actual pencil, soft enough to mark, hard enough not to smear. Marks are in the margin, not on the type. The glyph for "delete" is a ⌐, the glyph for "transpose" looks like a stylized infinity sign, and so on for every mark of the proofreading craft. None of this matters in software except as scaffolding for an argument: marginalia must be physically next to the type it annotates, or the metaphor evaporates.

"If your reader has to look across the room to see what you wrote about a paragraph, you have not built a press-check tool. You have built a comment thread."

Today's review surface centers a 700px column of text on a 1440px viewport, leaves the entire left half empty, and anchors a marginalia panel to the far right edge of the screen. The result is a column of prose floating in nothing, a cramped strip of notes anchored to the chrome of the window itself, and — at the bottom — a "scrapbook drawer" that looks like a drawer but actually navigates to a different page. Three architectural mistakes layered on top of each other.

The fix is composition, not polish

The redesign you are reading right now is the corrected composition: a single page object centered on the desk, holding both the article column AND the marginalia within its own bounds. The thin gutter rule between them is the same rule a typesetter would draw with a brass straightedge. Every margin note is a few centimeters from the prose it annotates, not a few hundred pixels.

  • The page is the unit, not the viewport.
  • The marginalia gutter is part of the page.
  • The desk is below the page; the chrome is above it.
  • The scrapbook is a drawer that opens in place.

None of this is novel. Every codex in human history has arranged itself this way. We just stopped doing it when the scroll wheel arrived.
