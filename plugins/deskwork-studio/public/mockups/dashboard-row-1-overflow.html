PatternThe row IS the primary affordance. Tap anywhere on the slug+title → review surface. The slug-link visual cue (underline, ink color) signals it's tappable. A single 32×32 ⋮ button on the right surfaces ALL secondary actions in a contextual menu — approve, iterate, cancel, scrapbook — based on the entry's state. Approve is at the top when reviewState is `in-review`; iterate when `iterating`. Scrapbook always present with item count.

Row height (default state)~64px regardless of how many actions an entry has. No chip wrap. Compare current implementation: 4-action row at 390px wraps to ~120px (3 lines of chips). On the visible Drafting expansion, this fits 4 rows in the same vertical budget that previously fit 2.

Trade-offApprove / iterate / cancel each become a 2-tap action: tap ⋮, then tap the action. Compared to the current 1-tap chip, that's a tap deeper. For high-frequency approve flows the operator may feel the friction. Open is still 1 tap (tap row body). The tradeoff is row density vs. action speed; the operator approves entries one-at-a-time, not in bulk, so the 2-tap is unlikely to be a bottleneck — but it IS a real cost.

When it winsOperator browses the dashboard to FIND the entry that needs attention, lands on it, opens the review surface to act. The dashboard's job in this flow is FIND, not act. Compact rows mean more rows visible per scroll. Once on the review surface, decisions live there inline (the entry-review surface already has approve/iterate/cancel buttons on its own chrome).

When it doesn'tOperator wants to triage from the dashboard without opening each entry — e.g., bulk-cancel three stale ideas. The 2-tap-per-action friction compounds. They could swipe (Direction C) faster.
