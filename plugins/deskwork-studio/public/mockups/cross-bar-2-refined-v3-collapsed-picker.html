Cross-bar 2 · refined v3 · collapsed destination picker.

One picker cell · opens a slide-up list of every studio destination · contextual region grows to 1–5 cells
Cross-bar 2 refinement v3 · operator's question
"What would it look like if, instead of enumerating the nav options, we collapsed them all to a single affordance that opens a list of choices?"
Like this. The bar's left side becomes one picker cell showing the current destination + a small chevron. Tap → a slide-up sheet listing every studio destination (Desk · Short · Manual · and any future addition). Pick one → navigate + sheet closes. The contextual region grows from v2-A's 1-4 cells to 1-5. The conceptual win is bigger than the cell-count math: the bar now has one job per region — the left is "where am I / go elsewhere," the right is "do work here," and the kraft hairline reads as a semantic divider, not just a structural rule.

§ 1 The picker cell

Single cell. Top: the current destination's glyph (rotates red when active). Bottom: the destination name + a small ▾ chevron. Tap behavior: opens a slide-up sheet listing every destination via Phase 2.1's createSlideUpSheet(). When the sheet is open, the chevron rotates 180° and the cell gets a red-pencil top inset rule + paper-2 background (the "I'm active" treatment, since you're picking from this cell).

Contextual region (1–5 cells) Up to 5 cells now (vs v2-A's 4, v1's 3). Surface-specific. Cell width at 6 cells / 390px = 65px. Entry-review can carry Outline · Notes · Scrap · Acts. · TOC simultaneously — every v0.18 affordance plus the still-missing #244 TOC drawer in one bar.
Sheet trigger Tap → Phase 2.1 createSlideUpSheet() opens with the destinations list. Same controller as Actions / Outline / Notes sheets. No new client-side primitive.
Active-state semantics Tapping the picker doesn't navigate — it opens the sheet. To navigate, the operator taps a row in the sheet. Tapping the currently-active destination in the sheet returns to that destination's root URL (useful when nested in a sub-surface like entry-review).
Chevron rotation The ▾ rotates 180° when the sheet is open. Single visual cue: cell + chevron + bar-state together communicate "menu open." Mirrors macOS / iOS popover patterns.

§ 5 What v3 unlocks

1 · TOC drawer on entry-review longform (closes #244 inline) v2-A could only fit TOC on shortform (where the 3-verb contextual stays under cap). v3 gives entry-review the 5th cell needed for a TOC tab — same operator-perceivable behavior as the longform outline-drawer, surfaced via the bar. Means #244 closes on BOTH review surfaces in one shape, not two.
2 · Shortform review carries Versions as a tab v2-A had no room for a Versions tab; the operator's only way to switch versions was a hidden gesture or a sub-page navigation. v3 fits Versions as the 6th cell (with a kraft-tone badge showing version count). Single-tap version switching.
3 · Destination namespace is unbounded v1/v2 capped destinations at the bar's nav-region width. Adding Folio (Phase 3), Content tree (Phase 3), or any future destination required widening the nav region or replacing existing tabs. v3 holds destinations in the picker sheet — adding one is just adding a row. Operator sees the namespace's shape in the sheet (including future destinations with phase tags) at all times.
4 · One job per region Cleanest conceptual model of the three refinements. Left region = "where am I / go elsewhere." Right region = "do work here." Operators don't have to learn which tabs do what — the kraft hairline IS the rule.

§ 6 What v3 costs

1 · Two-tap destination switching v1/v2 = one tap to switch destinations. v3 = tap picker → tap target = two taps. The cost is paid once per switch. For operators who switch destinations rarely (most), this is fine. For operators who cross-reference Desk ↔ Short mid-task, this adds friction every time. Mitigations:
  • Tap-and-hold on the picker could expose a "quick switch" gesture (skipping the sheet, going directly to the most-recent other destination). Not in the lead spec; reachable refinement.
  • Keyboard shortcut on desktop: cmd+1 Desk · cmd+2 Short · etc. Doesn't help on mobile but operators with keyboard often dock to switch.
2 · Destination list hidden at rest The operator can't see "Manual exists" until they open the picker. The chevron ▾ says more, not what. This is the canonical hamburger-menu critique. For domain-specific tools where the operator already knows the destinations (deskwork's case), it's tolerable. For a discovery-shaped surface (consumer apps), it's a known failure mode.
3 · Picker cell competes for the operator's eye In review surfaces with 5 contextual verbs, the picker becomes a 6th cell that doesn't do work. Some operators may tap it expecting an action and then learn it's nav. The visual weight (ink color, light paper bg) is calibrated to read as "title-like" but it's still a button. Real-device testing required.
4 · No at-a-glance signal of available destinations v1/v2 made all three destinations visible at all times — a wayfinding map. v3's affordance trades that map for a list-on-demand. Operators learn the namespace once, then the bar is mute about it. For the studio's small namespace (3-5 destinations), this is probably fine. For a larger namespace it would matter more.

D-2 trade-offs

Pro: Maximum bar real estate — 6 cells of pure work.
Pro: Tappable title IS the destination signifier — purest metaphor.
Con: Top-of-screen tap — worse thumb reach.
Con: Loses the kraft-divider's semantic split-bar identity (the v3 picker preserves it).
Con: The masthead title now does two jobs (label + control).
Con: Discoverability — the ▾ on a title is easy to miss; chevrons on bar cells read more clearly as "menu."

Worth naming so the operator can pick if they want it. Not the lead because the bar-picker cell holds the picker affordance bottom-anchored AND keeps the split-bar identity intact.

§ 8 Open questions for operator pick

Q1 · v2-A vs v3 · which trade do you take? v2-A: one-tap destination switching, 1-4 contextual cells, Scrapbook stays, TOC fits only on shortform.
v3: two-tap destination switching, 1-5 contextual cells, Scrapbook stays, TOC fits on both review surfaces, Versions tab fits on shortform, destination namespace unbounded.
Q2 · v3 picker label · destination name or just glyph? This mockup shows "Desk ▾" with both glyph and label. Alternative: just a glyph + ▾ chevron (cell is narrower; label is read from the masthead's kicker). Recommend: keep both — the explicit label reduces the cognitive load of "what am I in" when the masthead is dense.
Q3 · v3 vs D-2 (masthead-title tap)? D-2 lets the bar carry one MORE contextual cell (6 vs 5). Cost: tappable masthead title (worse thumb reach + chevron easier to miss). Most operators won't reach for it. If you want this for completeness in a future-state, mockup is partial and ready to drill into; if v3 is "good enough," stop here.
Q4 · Should future destinations show up in the picker sheet today? This mockup shows Folio (Phase 3) and Content tree (Phase 3) as dimmed rows with phase tags. Alternative: hide them entirely until they ship. Recommend: show with phase tags — the operator sees the namespace's shape and isn't surprised by future expansions. Dimmed rows are not promises; they're roadmap signals.
